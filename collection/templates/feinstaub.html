Particulate sensors


Particulates are solid or liquid particles with a diameter between 1 and 50 μm. They can cause many respatory illnesses such as lung cancer. Particulate pollution has become such a significant problem in many German cities that these cities have introduced low emission zones in which expecially polluting cars may not drive. And the emission rates are monitored with particulate matter sensors.

In all of Baden-Württemberg there are however only approx. 30 official particulate matter sensors - this is by far not enough to get an accurate picture of the situation! This ispired OK Lab Stuttgart - a group of volunteering engineers - to develop a cheap particulate matter sensor. To build your own particualte matter sensor, you can now follow their instructions, or better, attend a workshop where instructors help you build your sensor. In fact over 18 000 people have already done this!

These 18 000 sensors are producing a wealth of rich and interesting data!
Lets see what we can discover from it...

The Legal Limit


According to German law the average paricaluate pollution is not allowed to exceed 50 µg/m³ for more than 35 days a year.
(The european guidelines 2008/50/EG acctually suggest allowing no more than 7 days a year with pollution values exceeding 50 µg/m³.)

On the map below, you can see, that the situation is much grimmer than expected!!
It shows the locations where the average particulate pollution has exceeded 50 µg/m³ for more than 100 days in 2019 (01.01.2019 - 25.08.2019).
By moving the slider below the map, you can change the threshold to be any amount of days (not only 100).





Clustering


On the above map we see a large variation we can find some examples of different pollution values being measured by geographically very close sensors. There can different reasons for this, e.g. if a sensor is located directly beside a busy road, it will measure higher values than the one that is 100 meters away.

In order to get a more objective measure for the pollution, we therefore propose not looking at individual sensors, but at groups of sensors. By only considering the average pollution value of each group, the extreme values from individual sensors are averaged-out resulting in an objective measure for this area.

In order to split the sensors into local groups, we used a K-Means clustering algorithm. The visualisation below shows the sensors coloured according to their group affiliation.


In the following sections we will no longer consider the individual sensors and instead work with the groups/clusters.



New Years Eve

According to some sources, the new years fireworks in Germany release about 4 500 Tonns of particulate matter. This corresponds 15% of the annual emmisssions from traffic. We therefore expect to observe a spike in the measured paticulate matter pollution around new year.
